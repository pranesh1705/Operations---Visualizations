2 Assigning Probability
🎲

Classical Method

All outcomes equally likely: P(Eᵢ) = 1/n. Valid only when symmetry guarantees fairness — a fair die, a shuffled deck.

📊

Relative Frequency

P(Eᵢ) = (times Eᵢ occurred) / (total observations). Grounded in real data. Improves as sample size grows — the Law of Large Numbers.

🧠

Subjective Method

Expert judgment for unique, unrepeatable events. Different analysts may reasonably assign different probabilities — all are valid starting priors for Bayesian updating.

Inverse Problems — Rearranging the Addition Law

P(A∪B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A∩B) can be rearranged to find any unknown. Three worked business cases below.

Problem 1 — Find P(A)
Retail Customer Complaints

A customer survey finds that 42% of customers are unhappy with either store layout (L) or checkout speed (C). It is known that 14% complain about checkout speed and 8% complain about both. What fraction are unhappy specifically with the store layout?

Rearranged
P(L) = P(L∪C) − P(C) + P(L∩C) = 0.42 − 0.14 + 0.08 = 0.36
✅

36% of customers are dissatisfied with store layout. Cross-check: P(L∪C) = 0.36 + 0.14 − 0.08 = 0.42 ✓. Management should prioritise a store layout review.

Problem 2 — Find P(A∩B)
HR Compliance Audit

An HR audit at a 200-person firm shows that 38% of employees either missed a mandatory training session (T) or received a negative performance review (R). 22% missed training; 25% received a negative review. How many employees had both problems?

Rearranged
P(T∩R) = P(T) + P(R) − P(T∪R) = 0.22 + 0.25 − 0.38 = 0.09
✅

9% of staff — 0.09 × 200 = 18 employees — had both issues. These are the highest-risk individuals for the compliance team to address first.

Problem 3 — Verify Consistency
Logistics Operations Check

A logistics manager reports: 60% of shipments were late (L), 45% had damaged packaging (D), 15% had both, and 90% had at least one problem. Is this internally consistent?

Check
P(L∪D) = P(L) + P(D) − P(L∩D) = 0.60 + 0.45 − 0.15 = 0.90 ✓
✅

The numbers are perfectly consistent. Every figure the manager reported satisfies the Addition Law. If the reported union had been, say, 0.85, we could have immediately detected a data error: 0.60 + 0.45 − 0.15 ≠ 0.85.

Managerial Implications & Bridge to Bayes Theorem. The promotion rate for women (15%) is exactly half that for men (30%) — this is the discrimination evidence. But a second lens: P(Male|Promoted) = 88.9%. Men are 80% of the force yet account for 89% of promotions — they are over-represented among promotees relative to their workforce share. This is Bayes' theorem in action: a prior of 80% (share of force) updated by the evidence of promotion gives a posterior of 88.9%. We formalise this in the Bayes Theorem tab.

🔍 Mutually Exclusive vs Independent — Side-by-Side

Mutually Exclusive Events

Story: A product is classified as either Premium (P=0.35) or Standard (P=0.65). It cannot be both. P(Premium ∩ Standard) = 0.

⚠️

Mutually exclusive events with positive probability are always dependent. Here, P(Standard|Premium) = 0 ≠ P(Standard) = 0.65. Knowing Premium occurred makes Standard impossible — that is maximum dependence. One mathematical caveat: if P(A) = 0 or P(B) = 0, then P(A∩B) = 0 = P(A)×P(B) trivially holds — but a zero-probability event is of no practical interest. For any real business scenario where both events can occur, mutually exclusive implies dependent, without exception.

P(A∪B) = 0.35 + 0.65 = 1.00
P(A|B) = 0/0.65 = 0.00 ≠ P(A) → Dependent!
Independent Events

Story: Machine A passes QC (P=0.90) and Machine B passes QC (P=0.80). Machines operate independently.

✅

Independent events: knowing A passed tells us nothing about B. P(B|A) = P(B) = 0.80.

P(A∩B) = P(A) × P(B) = 0.90 × 0.80 = 0.72  ← the shaded overlap area
P(B|A) = 0.72/0.90 = 0.80 = P(B) → Independent ✓

6 Bayes' Theorem
🧠

The essence of Bayesian thinking: We start with a prior belief about the world. We observe new evidence. We update our belief using the likelihood of that evidence under each hypothesis. The result — the posterior — is a mathematically precise, rational update of our prior. Three business cases follow, increasing in complexity.

Bayes' Theorem
P(Aᵢ|B) = P(Aᵢ) × P(B|Aᵢ) / Σ[ P(Aⱼ) × P(B|Aⱼ) ]

⚙️ Supplier Quality — Who Sent the Defective Part?

Your factory sources components from two suppliers. Supplier 1 ships 65% of your parts with a 2% defect rate. Supplier 2 ships the remaining 35% but with a higher 5% defect rate. A machine just broke down due to a defective component. Which supplier most likely sent it? Before the breakdown you'd say 65% probability Supplier 1. After observing the defect, Bayes' theorem updates this based on each supplier's defect rate.

📖 Case 3 of 3
📋 The ₹40 Crore Government Contract

A management consulting firm has submitted a bid for a major government infrastructure project. Based on past bid outcomes, they estimate a 50/50 chance of winning. The government agency then requests additional technical documentation — a signal they historically send for 75% of bids they plan to accept, and 40% of bids they plan to reject. The request has just arrived. What is the updated probability of winning? And of losing? Note that both posteriors must sum to exactly 1.000 — a useful check of your arithmetic.

Case 3 — Consulting Bid: Update Both Posteriors
P(Win | Info requested)65.2%
P(Lose | Info requested)34.8%
P(Info) = 0.575
P(Win|Info) = P(Win)×P(Info|Win) / P(Info)
= 0.5×0.75 / 0.575 = 0.652
Sum check: 0.652 + 0.348 = 1.000 ✓
📈

Prior belief: 50% chance of winning. After the info request signal: 65.2% — the evidence improves your estimate. This is Bayesian updating in action.
